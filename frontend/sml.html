01 Descriptive Statistics — The Foundation

Interview Answer "Walk me through the statistics of your dataset." — Mean tells you the centre, median tells you where most values actually sit, std tells you the spread, and skewness tells you the shape. When mean > median, the distribution is right-skewed — a small number of high-paying roles pull the average up.
Skewness measures the asymmetry of a distribution. Positive skewness means right-skewed — most salaries cluster low with a tail of high earners. Why it matters: if you use the mean to describe a "typical" salary, you're misleading — the median is more honest. Always report both in interviews. The formula: skewness = Σ((x - μ) / σ)³ / n

Model Note This is a rule-based model, not a trained ML model. The baselines and multipliers come directly from averaging our ...-row dataset grouped by role and city. It's transparent and explainable — which is actually more valuable than a black-box model for this dataset size.
Rule-based models use explicit logic derived from domain knowledge or data averages. They're interpretable, fast, and work well with small datasets.

ML models learn patterns automatically. They outperform rule-based when: (1) you have enough data (1000+ rows), (2) relationships are non-linear, (3) there are many interacting features.

With a limited number of unique salary values, an ML model here would overfit. The honest choice is the rule-based approach with a clear caveat.

OLS finds the line minimising squared residuals.

slope (β₁) = Σ(x - x̄)(y - ȳ) / Σ(x - x̄)²
intercept (β₀) = ȳ - β₁ × x̄

R² = 1 - (SS_residual / SS_total). 1 = perfect fit. 0 = explains nothing.

Running Z-test...
Hypothesis testing asks: "Could this difference have happened by chance?" The p-value is the probability of seeing this result if H₀ were true. p < 0.05 → reject H₀.
Test 2: Bangalore vs Other Cities
H₀: Bangalore salaries are not significantly different from other cities.
H₁: Bangalore offers significantly different salaries.
α: 0.05
Running Z-test...
Z-test: n > 30, population std known. T-test: n < 30 or std unknown.

Formula: Z = (x̄₁ - x̄₂) / SE where SE = √(σ₁²/n₁ + σ₂²/n₂)
Interview Answer "Have you done hypothesis testing?" — Yes. I tested whether Data Engineers earn significantly more. Large Z-statistic, p < 0.001 — rejected the null at 99% confidence. I also tested Bangalore vs other cities and found no significant difference — role matters more than location.

Common interview questions
Q"What's the difference between MAE and RMSE?" — MAE treats all errors equally. RMSE penalises large errors more. Use RMSE when large errors are costly; MAE for interpretable average error.
Q"Your R² is low — is your model useless?" — Not necessarily. Low R² told me experience isn't the driver. Role category explains ~72% of variance — that's the real finding.
Q"How would you improve this model?" — Get individually-reported salaries from Glassdoor/AmbitionBox. Add features: company size, skills count, remote vs on-site. With richer data, use Linear Regression or Gradient Boosting.
MAE = average |actual - predicted|. Easy to interpret.

RMSE = √(avg (actual - predicted)²). Higher than MAE, penalises big mistakes.

R²: 0.9+ great, 0.5–0.9 acceptable, below 0.5 weak.
The Honest Takeaway A good analyst knows when their model is limited — and says so clearly. The rule-based predictor works well for role-level estimation. The regression fails because of data granularity — a data problem, not a model problem. Knowing the difference is what separates a junior from a good analyst.
